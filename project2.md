**1. Multi-scale investigation of SFE at high-redshift galaxies: universal star formation revealed in Thesan-Zoom**

Cover: SFE.png

https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2025MNRAS.544.2675W/abstract

Ever since JWST revealed an excess of massive galaxies at high redshift, both theoretical and observational studies have increasingly argued for an elevated baryon conversion efficiency in the early universe. However, understanding star formation in galaxies is inherently complex, as it emerges from the interplay of multiple physical scales and processes. Therefore, it is essential to examine which scales and mechanisms are responsible for the apparent increase in global star formation efficiency (SFE).

In this work, we extend the analysis of [Shen2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.01949) to investigate the connection between **global (galaxy-scale)** and **local (cloud-scale)** SFE at high redshift, using the state-of-the-art cosmological zoom-in simulation suite, thesan-zoom.

We find that **star formation in these systems is predominantly hosted by filamentary giant molecular clouds (GMCs)** embedded in dense interstellar media (ISM) and subjected to strong external pressure. At the GMC scale, **star formation appears universal across redshifts and halo masses**. GMCs maintain a nearly constant **gas surface density**, while their **dark matter surface density increases with redshift**, leading to the emergence of **DM-dominated GMCs at $z > 10$**, which could exhibit elevated SFEs. Moreover, we find that the **GMC mass fraction** is the dominant factor shaping the global SFR–surface density relation.

Building upon this cloud data, we also explain the emergence of nitrogen-rich galaxies (https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2026MNRAS.548ag016M/abstract) and the potential origin of the Multi-Population chemical feature in globular clusters (https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05509).



**2. Clumpiness of galaxies across wavelengths, redshifts, and galaxy formation models **

Cover: clumps.png

Link coming soon.

[Copy from abstract, please rearrange this:] Recent JWST observations have revealed the diverse morphology of high-redshift galaxies, with a population of galaxies exhibiting irregular and clumpy structures. The physical nature of these clumpy structures and potential observational biases are yet to be understood. In this work, we present a power-spectrum-based approach to quantify galaxy clumpiness across different spatial scales, based on the zoom-in radiation-hydrodynamic simulation suite Thesan-Zoom that employs a state-of-the-art galaxy formation model resolving the multiphase interstellar medium (ISM). We find that \thesanzoom galaxies exhibit smooth intrinsic stellar mass distributions over cosmic time. In contrast, the power spectra of emission fields (H$\alpha$, FUV, and optical emission) show systematically different patterns. Tracers sensitive to shorter star formation timescales exhibit more pronounced small-scale structure ($\sim10^{2}-10^{3}\rm pc$), providing a natural connection to the clumps observed by JWST. These spectra follow approximate power-law scalings, $P(k)\propto k^{-1}$ to $k^{-2}$, with shallower slopes for shorter-timescale tracers, indicating enhanced small-scale power and stronger spatial intermittency in young star-forming regions. This behaviour is consistent with a highly compressible, shock-dominated ISM, where stellar feedback and outflows can reorganize dense gas into filamentary and clumpy structures. We further show that galaxy clumpiness is highly sensitive to the treatment of stellar feedback, with weaker early stellar feedback leading to enhanced small-scale power in both mass and light distributions. Both mass and light clumpiness vary strongly across phases of bursty star formation, suggesting that the clumpy galaxies identified in current observations may be biased toward periods of elevated star formation activity.
