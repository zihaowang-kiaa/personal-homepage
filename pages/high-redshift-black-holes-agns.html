Why LRDs

They may be the first visible spots where galaxy formation and black hole growth meet.

Little Red Dots are not just another JWST population. They are small, red, abundant, and possibly overmassive enough to stress how modern cosmological simulations build the early Universe.

Observationally, LRDs appear as one of the most striking discoveries in the JWST era: compact sources with a blue UV component, a red optical component, broad-line signatures in some cases, and inferred black-hole-to-stellar mass ratios that can be unexpectedly high.

Their abundance and compactness make them difficult to treat as a footnote. If they are powered by black holes, they may expose how massive seeds form and grow. If they are compact star-forming systems, they still demand extreme early star formation. Either way, theory has to model their origin, nature, and evolution.

Working with Prof. Kohei Inayoshi and Fangzhou Jiang, I am developing this as a connected and expanding program: use clustering to constrain the initial conditions, model how a gas-enshrouded black hole seed might transition toward a blue dot or normal AGN, and keep opening new theoretical branches as observations sharpen.

01

Overly abundant

LRDs may represent a surprisingly large fraction of high-redshift galaxy populations, making number density itself a theoretical constraint.

02

Overly massive

If broad-line interpretations trace accreting black holes, some systems imply unusually high black-hole-to-host ratios.

03

Ambiguous identity

The same red compact source can be read as an obscured AGN, a gas-enshrouded seed, or an extreme compact galaxy.

04

Simulation challenge

Modern simulations need subgrid and initial-condition models capable of producing compact, abundant, rapidly evolving systems.

Research threads

A growing program: from environment to nuclei, with new branches open.

The cards below are current entry points rather than a closed list. The aim is to keep extending the same question across halo environments, black-hole growth, compact star formation, feedback, and simulation models.

Current thread / published

Clustering & Bias

One entry point asks whether large-scale environments can distinguish competing LRD formation channels.

  • Characterizes early-Universe halo assembly bias in the Shin-Uchuu cosmological N-body simulation.
  • Tracks how formation time, concentration, and spin change clustering at fixed mass, and how those signals evolve toward high redshift.
  • Applies the bias model to LRD scenarios: direct-collapse black holes, low-spin compact galaxies, SIDM core-collapse haloes, and primordial black holes.
  • Finds that the DCBH scenario predicts the strongest large-scale bias and enhanced pair fractions, while low-spin/SIDM scenarios are weaker and PBH-like initiation is close to unbiased.

Current thread / ongoing

Cooling-envelope transition

Another thread follows the inner engine: how a gas-enshrouded black hole seed can move from a red, cool envelope phase toward a Little Blue Dot or normal AGN.

  • Models a weakly supplied gas envelope around a growing black hole, with feedback regulating envelope loss.
  • Maps the parameter space where an LRD-like phase can survive, including effective temperatures around the red-envelope regime.
  • Finds that envelope mass-loss rate and BH feedback efficiency control whether the system makes a rapid or long transition.
  • Uses the observed contrast between LRDs, Little Blue Dots, and normal AGNs as a way to constrain feedback prescriptions.

Framework

From a dot to a biography: initial conditions, nuclei, stars, galaxies.

01

Initial conditions

Halo mass, concentration, spin, UV-bright companions, and metal contamination set the boundary conditions for LRD formation.

02

Nuclei transition

A gas-enshrouded black hole seed evolves through an envelope-regulated phase before becoming less obscured.

03

Star formation

The same compact environment may host extreme star formation, nuclear star clusters, or feedback-regulated bursts.

04

Galaxy formation

LRDs become a stress test for how simulations connect early disks, feedback, quenching, and high-redshift observables.

The long-term goal is a model where Little Red Dots are not isolated anomalies, but a readable phase in the coupled formation of early galaxies and massive black holes.
