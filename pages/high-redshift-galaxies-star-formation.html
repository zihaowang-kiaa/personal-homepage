Published / galaxy and cloud scales

Multi-scale investigation of star formation efficiency at high redshift

Author list: Zihao Wang, Xuejian Shen, Mark Vogelsberger, Hui Li, Rahul Kannan, Ewald Puchwein, Aaron Smith, Josh Borrow, Enrico Garaldi, Laura Keating, Oliver Zier, William McClymont, Sandro Tacchella, Yang Ni, Lars Hernquist

The work connects global galaxy-scale star formation efficiency to local cloud-scale physics, asking which scales are responsible for apparently elevated baryon conversion efficiency at high redshift.

  • Star formation is predominantly hosted by filamentary giant molecular clouds embedded in dense, high-pressure interstellar media.
  • At the GMC scale, star formation appears nearly universal across redshift and halo mass.
  • GMC gas surface density remains nearly constant, while dark matter surface density rises toward high redshift.
  • At z > 10, dark-matter-dominated GMCs can emerge and may exhibit elevated star formation efficiencies.
  • The GMC mass fraction is the dominant factor shaping the global SFR-surface density relation.

Developing / morphology and feedback

Clumpiness across wavelengths, redshifts, and galaxy formation models

Author list: Zihao Wang, collaborators

Recent JWST observations reveal irregular and clumpy high-redshift galaxies. This work uses a power-spectrum-based approach to quantify clumpiness across spatial scales in simulations.

  • Intrinsic stellar mass distributions remain comparatively smooth over cosmic time.
  • Emission fields such as H alpha, FUV, and optical light show systematically stronger small-scale structure.
  • Shorter-timescale star-formation tracers show shallower power spectra and enhanced spatial intermittency on roughly 100-1000 pc scales.
  • Clumpiness is sensitive to stellar feedback prescriptions and varies strongly across bursty star-formation phases.
  • The contrast between clumpy light and smooth stellar mass helps separate intrinsic galaxy structure from observational timescale bias.
